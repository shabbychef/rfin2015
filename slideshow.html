Why does this happen?

Why overfit happens

For 'bad' quants:

  • Plain old overfit ('p-value hacking').
  • Broken backtests: lookahead bias, survivorship bias, etc.
  • Bad understanding of trade costs.
  • Bad execution.

For 'good' quants:

  • Bad luck.
  • A fundamental bound.

A fundamental bound?

Consider portfolio estimator as function:

  • The input is historical data, the \(T\times p\) matrix \(X\), say.
  • The output is the portfolio, a \(p\) vector.

Consider the (population) Sharpe ratio of this portfolio, its expected value divided by its volatility.

We will bound the expected Sharpe, with expectation over draws of \(X\).

The bound

\[ \mbox{Cramer Rao} + \mbox{geometry} + \mbox{math} => \mbox{bound on expected Sharpe}. \]

Under a wide range of conditions the bound is: \[ E_X\left[Sharpe\right] \le \sqrt{\frac{\mbox{effect size}}{\mbox{# knobs} + \mbox{effect size}}} \mbox{maximal Sharpe}, \] with \(\mbox{effect size} = \mbox{# years}\, (\mbox{maximal Sharpe})^2.\)

Control your free variables

This is why portfolio optimization is not performed on 100's of unknowns:

If maximal Sharpe is \(1.1\), observing \(5 \mbox{yr}\) of data:

  • for \(10\) stocks, the bound is \(0.7\).
  • for \(40\) stocks, the bound is \(0.4\).
  • for \(160\) stocks, the bound is \(0.21\).

However, maximal Sharpe should grow with universe size. Can it grow fast enough?

Diversification and universe size
